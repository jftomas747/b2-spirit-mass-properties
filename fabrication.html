Fabrication process

From 3D printed PLA core to finished carbon fiber shell.

1. PLA core assembly and surface preparation

The process began by assembling the 3D printed PLA core sections using steel-infused glue. After assembly, the entire model was sanded to remove sharp polygon-looking print surfaces and to create a smoother base for the carbon fiber. This step mattered because the carbon shell would show surface flaws if the core was left too rough.

Hand-drawn layup stack showing the vacuum bag, breather, release film, and carbon layer.

2. Material preparation

Before the wet layup started, the carbon fiber fabric was cut with extra margin around the aircraft outline. Other materials were also prepared in advance, including the vacuum sheet, gum tape, peel ply, blue breathable release film, and thick cotton breather material.

Pre-cutting and organizing the consumables first made the layup easier because the fabric and bagging layers could be added quickly once the epoxy was mixed.

3. Bottom-side carbon fiber layup

The layup was done one side at a time, starting with the bottom. A 1:4 hardener-to-epoxy mixture was applied while pressing the carbon fiber into the aircraft surface by hand. Extra effort was needed around curves, wing edges, the nose, and the raised duct features to remove bubbles and improve contact.

One side of the aircraft sealed under the vacuum bag during cure.
Vacuum pump connected to the sealed bag during the cure cycle.

4. Vacuum bagging and cure

After the carbon layer was in place, the blue release film, peel ply, breather, and vacuum bag were added. A vacuum puck was placed on top, then the bag was sealed with gum tape and connected to the pump. The bagged part was left to cure for about 72 hours.

After the bottom side cure. Carbon fiber had not been added to the top yet, and stuck bagging material still had to be removed.
Cleanup and trimming after the first layup, preparing the surface for the top-side carbon fiber layup.

5. Full sanding and finish coat

After both sides were laid up and cured, the whole aircraft was sanded again to smooth out epoxy buildup, trim edges, and prepare the surface for the final seal coat. The sanded model had a matte appearance at this stage. A thin final epoxy coat was then applied to restore the carbon weave appearance and create the glossy finished look.

Sanded matte finish before applying the thin final epoxy layer.
Completed model after the final thin epoxy coat.

Fabrication outcome

The finished part was larger, stiffer, and visually cleaner than the earlier small B-2 model used in the lab. The carbon weave remained visible, and the overall shape was suitable for repeated mass properties demonstrations.
