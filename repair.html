Repair and spare-model production

How the damaged aircraft was restored and why additional spares were requested.

Student-use damage and repair

During student lab experimentation, one of the B-2 Spirit models broke near the nose. The damaged carbon fiber split was repaired by injecting cyanoacrylate into the crack and holding the area closed by hand for about 10 minutes. After the crack was closed, aircraft-grade structural adhesive was added at the tip and allowed to cure.

The repaired area was then sanded to remove excess adhesive. Acrylic paint was applied carefully to match the carbon fiber appearance as closely as possible, and the repaired zone was sealed with a thin 1:4 hardener-to-epoxy mixture.

Close-up of the repaired nose area after bonding, sanding, and sealing.

Spare models for the laboratory

Because the original group of models turned out well and were actively used in the Mechanical Vibrations laboratory, Dr. Hemanth Porumamilla later asked for spare B-2 Spirit models to be produced for continued lab use. This showed that the original project was successful enough to justify repeated fabrication.

Two finished models prepared for lab use, helping motivate spare production.

Why spares matter

  • Student laboratory equipment gets handled often and can be damaged over time.
  • Having spares reduces downtime for the experiment.
  • Repeated builds also improve consistency in the manufacturing process.
  • The repair experience helped prove the model was maintainable, not just buildable.
