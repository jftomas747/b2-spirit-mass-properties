Fabrication, finishing, repair, and spare production of a carbon fiber B-2 Spirit model

This website summarizes the upgraded 3.5-foot B-2 Spirit model built for the Mechanical Vibrations Mass Properties Lab at Cal Poly. The project replaced an older 1-foot model with a larger 3D-printed PLA core and carbon fiber shell produced through wet layup, vacuum bagging, surface finishing, repair work, and continued spare-model production.

Overview

Project at a glance

The upgraded B-2 Spirit model was designed to serve as a more useful and visible aircraft-shaped test object for mass properties demonstrations involving center of gravity, natural frequency, and mass-moment-of-inertia in the Mechanical Vibrations laboratory.

1 ft → 3.5 ft
The older lab model was replaced with a much larger version that is easier to handle and demonstrate.
~1:50 scale
The new B-2 Spirit model is close to a 1:50 scale representation of the actual aircraft.
3 built + 2 more
Three models were completed during Summer 2024, and two additional spare models are being made for future lab use.

Main materials and methods

3D-printed PLA core sections were joined with steel-infused glue, surface-prepped by sanding, and then wrapped in carbon fiber using wet layup and vacuum bagging. After cure, the model was trimmed, re-sanded, and sealed with a thin epoxy coat.

Why the upgrade mattered

The larger aircraft improves visibility during lab demonstrations and creates a better-looking and stiffer piece of equipment while still serving the same educational purpose in the mass properties experiment.

Background

Purpose of the model in the lab

In the Mass Properties experiment, irregularly shaped objects are used to help students connect physical geometry with center of gravity, natural frequency, and mass-moment-of-inertia. The B-2 model acts as one of those non-simple shapes, so this project focused on creating a stronger and more refined replacement for that lab equipment.

Design direction

A PLA core made the complex aircraft geometry practical to build, while the carbon fiber outer shell improved stiffness, appearance, and durability. Wet layup and vacuum bagging were chosen to pull the laminate more tightly against the model and reduce trapped air.

Build goals

  • Create a larger, cleaner, and more realistic B-2 Spirit model for the lab.
  • Preserve the educational value of the original mass properties setup.
  • Develop a repeatable process for future spare-model production.

This site focuses on the manufacturing story: core preparation, composite layup, finishing, repair, and the follow-on work to keep the Mechanical Vibrations laboratory supplied with spare B-2 models.

Fabrication

How the model was built

The fabrication process was done in stages so the complex B-2 geometry could be controlled more carefully. One side was laid up and cured first, then trimmed and cleaned before the top side was prepared and finished.

01

PLA core assembly and surface prep

The 3D-printed PLA sections were aligned and bonded together using steel-infused glue. After assembly, the surface was sanded heavily to remove polygon-like print geometry and soften features that would otherwise show through the carbon fiber shell.

02

Material setup and wet layup

Carbon fiber was cut with extra margin around a rough B-2 outline. The vacuum sheet, peel ply, blue breathable release film, breather material, and gum tape were prepared first so the layup could be sealed quickly once the epoxy was worked into the fabric. A 1:4 hardener-to-epoxy ratio was used during the layup process.

03

Vacuum cure and first-side completion

The bottom side of the model was laid up first. After the bag was sealed, the part was connected to the pump and left to cure for about 72 hours. The vacuum setup helped press the laminate tighter against the aircraft contours while reducing trapped air.

04

Cleanup after the bottom side was finished

These next two images show an intermediate stage. The bottom side was already cured, but the top side did not have carbon fiber on yet. Stuck bagging material had to be trimmed away and the surface had to be cleaned before the next layup on the top side.

05

Full sanding and matte finish stage

After both sides were laid up, the whole model was re-sanded to flatten resin buildup, smooth edges, and prepare the surface for the final thin sealing coat. At this stage the aircraft had a matte look, which was expected and part of the finishing process.

06

Final thin epoxy coat and glossy finish

A thin final coat of the same epoxy system was applied after sanding. The goal was not to flood the surface, but to seal it lightly, bring out the carbon fiber weave, and create a cleaner finished appearance for lab use.

Results

Main outcomes of the project

The project produced a larger, stiffer, and more refined aircraft model for the Mechanical Vibrations laboratory while also developing a process that could be repeated for future builds.

Improved lab hardware

The upgraded 3.5-foot model replaced a much smaller aircraft and created a stronger visual presence for demonstrations and student interaction.

Composite finish quality

The final seal coat made the weave easier to see and gave the model a glossy surface that looked much more complete than the sanded intermediate state.

Repeatable process

By building multiple models, the layup, cleanup, sanding, and finishing sequence became much more repeatable for continued lab support.

By the end of Summer 2024, three B-2 Spirit models had been completed. The success of those builds directly led to additional repair work and a request to create spare models for the laboratory.

Repair

Repairing a damaged B-2 model after lab use

One of the B-2 Spirit models was damaged during student lab experimentation. The repair process focused on restoring the split area cleanly enough that the damaged region would be hard to notice once the surface was blended and sealed.

Repair method

  • Cyanoacrylate was injected into the split carbon fiber.
  • The damaged region was hand-clamped for about 10 minutes.
  • Aircraft-grade structural adhesive was added at the tip and allowed to cure.
  • Excess adhesive was sanded away before an acrylic paint touch-up was applied.
  • A thin 1:4 hardener-to-epoxy seal coat finished the repair.

Why this mattered

Because the model is handled during student experiments, durability and repairability matter almost as much as initial fabrication quality. This repair showed that the model could be restored and returned to lab use instead of being discarded.

Continuation

Spare-model production for the Mechanical Vibrations lab

After the original set of models proved useful in the laboratory, Dr. Hemanth Porumamilla asked for spare B-2 Spirit models to be made for continued Mechanical Vibrations lab use. That request turned the project from a one-time build into an ongoing lab-support effort.

What the spare builds show

The request for spares indicates that the upgraded model was successful enough to keep reproducing. It also shows that the fabrication process had become consistent enough to support more than a single finished part.

Project continuation

In addition to the three completed Summer 2024 models, two more B-2 builds are being created using the same general composite process so the lab can keep backup models ready for future classes.
